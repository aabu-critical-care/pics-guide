  • Critical Care

  • Introduction

    Advances in critical care medicine have significantly reduced mortality rates among critically ill patients. As a result, the number of intensive care unit (ICU) survivors has increased worldwide. Despite improved survival, many patients experience persistent health problems after discharge from the ICU. These long-term complications are collectively known as Post-Intensive Care Syndrome (PICS). PICS represents a major public health concern due to its impact on patients’ functional status, quality of life, and healthcare utilization.
    Post-ICU recovery is often complex and prolonged. Patients and their families frequently report feeling unprepared for the physical, cognitive, and psychological challenges that emerge after discharge. In low- and middle-income countries, including Jordan, post-ICU follow-up services remain limited, making patient education a critical component of care. Understanding PICS and its management is therefore essential for nurses and other healthcare professionals involved in critical care and post-discharge planning.

  • Definition and Concept of Post-Intensive Care Syndrome

    Post-Intensive Care Syndrome is defined as new or worsening impairments in physical, cognitive, or mental health status that arise after critical illness and persist beyond ICU discharge (Needham et al., 2012). These impairments may continue for months or years and can significantly limit a patient’s ability to return to pre-illness levels of functioning.
    PICS does not represent a single disease but rather a constellation of interrelated conditions. The syndrome may affect patients, their families, or both. Family members may develop psychological distress, a phenomenon sometimes referred to as PICS-Family (PICS-F). This highlights the broad and multidimensional impact of critical illness beyond the ICU setting.

  • Epidemiology and Risk Factors

    Studies suggest that up to 50–70% of ICU survivors experience at least one component of PICS (Inoue et al., 2019). The prevalence varies depending on patient characteristics, severity of illness, and duration of ICU stay.
    Major risk factors include:

    • Prolonged ICU admission
    • Mechanical ventilation, especially long-term ventilation
    • Sepsis and septic shock
    • Delirium during ICU stay
    • Advanced age
    • Pre-existing chronic diseases
    • Deep or prolonged sedation
    • Immobility and bed rest

    These factors are commonly encountered in ICU patients, making PICS a frequent but often underdiagnosed condition.

  • Pathophysiology of PICS

    The pathophysiology of PICS is multifactorial and not fully understood. Physical impairments are primarily related to ICU-acquired weakness, which results from muscle wasting, systemic inflammation, immobility, and critical illness polyneuropathy or myopathy.
    Cognitive impairments are associated with hypoxia, hypotension, inflammation, metabolic disturbances, and ICU delirium. Neuroinflammation and microvascular injury may contribute to long-term cognitive dysfunction.
    Psychological impairments arise from the traumatic nature of critical illness, frightening ICU experiences, sleep deprivation, and loss of control. Sedation, mechanical ventilation, and delirium further increase the risk of anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

  • Clinical Manifestations of PICS

    .1 Physical Impairments
    Physical symptoms include generalized weakness, fatigue, reduced endurance, impaired balance, and difficulty performing activities of daily living. Many patients experience decreased exercise tolerance and prolonged dependence on caregivers. These impairments significantly affect independence and social reintegration.
    .2 Cognitive Impairments
    Cognitive deficits may involve memory impairment, reduced attention span, difficulty concentrating, and impaired executive function. These symptoms can interfere with medication adherence, decision-making, and return to work. Cognitive dysfunction following ICU admission has been compared to mild traumatic brain injury or early dementia in severity.
    .3 Psychological Impairments
    Psychological symptoms are common and include anxiety, depression, and PTSD. Patients may experience nightmares, flashbacks, mood changes, irritability, and emotional numbness. These symptoms often coexist with physical and cognitive impairments, compounding their impact on quality of life.

  • Management and Treatment of PICS

    There is no single treatment for PICS; management requires a multidisciplinary and individualized approach sEarly recognition and intervention are key to improving outcomes.
    Key management strategies include:

    • Early mobilization and physical rehabilitation
    • Occupational therapy to support functional recovery
    • Cognitive rehabilitation and mental stimulation
    • Psychological support and counseling
    • Pharmacological management when indicated
    • Structured ICU follow-up clinics

    Rehabilitation should begin as early as possible, ideally during ICU admission, and continue after discharge.

  • Role of Nurses in PICS Prevention and Management

    Nurses play a central role in preventing and managing PICS. During ICU admission, nurses contribute to prevention through early mobilization, delirium screening, sedation management, and family engagement. After discharge, nurses are essential in patient education, symptom monitoring, and coordination of follow-up care.
    Nurse-led education empowers patients and families to recognize symptoms early and seek timely medical attention. In resource-limited settings, such as many Jordanian hospitals, nurses are often the primary source of post-discharge guidance.

  • Importance of Patient and Family Education

    Patient education is a cornerstone of PICS management. Educating patients and families about expected symptoms, recovery timelines, self-care strategies, and warning signs improves safety and reduces anxiety. Clear instructions on when to contact healthcare providers enhance early detection of complications and prevent unnecessary hospital readmissions.
    Educational tools should be culturally appropriate, easy to understand, and tailored to the patient’s literacy level. In Jordan, providing education in Arabic with simple terminology improves accessibility and adherence.

  • Implications for Nursing Practice in Jordan

    In Jordanian hospitals, post-ICU care remains fragmented, and formal follow-up clinics are limited. This places greater responsibility on nurses to ensure continuity of care through discharge planning and patient education. Integrating PICS awareness into nursing practice can improve long-term patient outcomes and reduce the burden on healthcare systems.
    Developing structured educational tools and discharge protocols focused on PICS is particularly relevant in military and public hospitals, where ICU patient volumes are high.

  • Conclusion

    Post-Intensive Care Syndrome is a prevalent and impactful condition affecting ICU survivors worldwide. It encompasses physical, cognitive, and psychological impairments that can persist long after hospital discharge. Early identification, multidisciplinary management, and comprehensive patient education are essential to improving recovery and quality of life. Nurses play a vital role in addressing PICS, particularly in settings with limited post-ICU resources. Increasing awareness and integrating patient-focused education into routine care are critical steps toward better long-term outcomes for ICU survivors.
